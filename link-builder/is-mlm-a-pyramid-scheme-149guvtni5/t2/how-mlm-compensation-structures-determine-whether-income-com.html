How MLM Compensation Structures Determine Whether Income Comes From Product Sales Or Recruitment

Multi-level marketing compensation plans set the rules for how a participant earns money, and those rules are what ultimately reveal whether a program rewards selling products or recruiting people. In a plan built around retail sales, distributors earn primarily by selling goods to actual customers, and any bonuses tied to a "downline" of recruits are paid on the product those recruits sell to end users. In a recruitment-driven plan, by contrast, the bulk of a participant's earnings depends on signing up new members and on the fees or required product purchases those members make when they join, rather than on genuine demand from outside customers. The mechanical details of the plan — how commissions are calculated, what triggers a bonus, and whether qualifying for income requires ongoing personal purchases — are therefore the clearest signal of which model a company actually runs.

Regulators tend to look past a company's marketing language and examine where the money originates: if income can only be sustained by continuously enrolling new participants, the structure resembles a pyramid scheme regardless of whether a product technically exists. Features such as large upfront buy-ins, monthly purchase quotas to stay "active," and commissions paid on recruitment rather than on sales to real consumers are commonly treated as warning signs. A legitimate direct-sales plan, in principle, can pay everyone in the network from a steady stream of retail purchases by people who never join, while a recruitment-dependent plan collapses once enrollment slows because there is no outside revenue holding it up. Reading a compensation plan carefully — asking where each dollar of commission comes from — is the practical way to tell the two apart.
