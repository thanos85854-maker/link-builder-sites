Legal Test: How The FTC Distinguishes MLMs From Illegal Pyramid Schemes

The Federal Trade Commission's core legal test turns on where the money actually comes from. In a legitimate multi-level marketing company, participants earn income primarily by selling a genuine product or service to real customers outside the network. In an illegal pyramid scheme, the rewards flow mainly from recruiting new participants — the payments to those at the top depend on an endless supply of newcomers buying in, rather than on any actual retail demand. Regulators therefore ask whether a distributor's compensation is tied to retail sales to end users or to the act of enrolling others. When recruitment is the real engine of earnings, the structure is treated as a pyramid, because it mathematically must collapse once recruitment slows and leaves the newest members with losses.

Several practical signals feed into that determination. The FTC examines whether commissions are paid on genuine sales to people outside the distributor network, whether participants are pressured to buy large amounts of inventory they cannot realistically resell (often called inventory loading), and whether "sales" are really just distributors purchasing product to qualify for bonuses rather than serving outside demand. Courts and regulators have also scrutinized whether internal consumption by distributors can honestly count as retail sales at all. No single factor is decisive; the assessment weighs the compensation plan as a whole to see whether it rewards selling or merely recruiting.
