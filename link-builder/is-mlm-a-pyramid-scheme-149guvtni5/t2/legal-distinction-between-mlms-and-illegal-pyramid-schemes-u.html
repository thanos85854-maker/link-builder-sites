Legal Distinction Between MLMs And Illegal Pyramid Schemes Under FTC Criteria

The core legal test the Federal Trade Commission applies looks at where a participant's income actually comes from. A legitimate multi-level marketing company generates its distributors' earnings primarily from selling real products or services to genuine end customers, whereas an illegal pyramid scheme rewards participants mainly for recruiting new members, with product sales serving as little more than a cover for what is essentially a money-transfer chain. Under this framework, the presence of a real product is not by itself enough to make a company lawful; regulators look at whether compensation is driven by retail demand or by the act of enrolling others. When most of the money flows upward from recruitment fees and mandatory inventory purchases rather than from customers who want the goods, the operation tends to be treated as an unlawful pyramid regardless of how it labels itself.

Courts and the FTC have developed this distinction through enforcement cases over the years, and the analysis typically weighs several practical factors: whether participants must buy large amounts of inventory to qualify for commissions, whether products are sold to outside customers or merely consumed by distributors themselves, and whether the company will buy back unsold stock. Because pyramid schemes are mathematically unsustainable — each tier depends on ever-larger recruitment below it — they eventually collapse and leave the majority of later joiners with losses. The upshot is that the legality of an MLM turns not on its marketing structure, which can look similar in both cases, but on the underlying economics of how participants are actually paid.
