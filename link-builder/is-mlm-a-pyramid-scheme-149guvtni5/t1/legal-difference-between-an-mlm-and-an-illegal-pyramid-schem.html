Legal Difference Between An MLM And An Illegal Pyramid Scheme Under The FTC Retail-Vs-Recruitment Test

The legal line between a multi-level marketing company and an illegal pyramid scheme turns on where the money actually comes from. A legitimate MLM sells a real product or service to genuine end customers, and the bulk of a participant's earnings is supposed to trace back to those retail sales. An illegal pyramid scheme, by contrast, pays participants primarily for recruiting new members rather than for moving product to people outside the sales network. Regulators focus on this because a business that rewards recruitment above sales has no sustainable source of revenue: it depends on an ever-expanding base of new recruits, and it collapses once recruitment slows, leaving those who joined late with losses. The Federal Trade Commission has articulated this "retail versus recruitment" distinction through its enforcement actions and guidance.

In practice, the distinction is harder to apply than to state, because many arrangements mix real sales with recruitment incentives. Courts and the FTC tend to look past how a company labels itself and instead examine its actual compensation structure: whether rewards flow chiefly from selling to genuine customers or from signing up distributors and requiring them to buy inventory. Warning signs of an unlawful scheme include large upfront buy-ins, pressure to purchase more product than one can realistically sell, and bonuses tied to headcount rather than to consumer demand. Because the same company can sit on either side of the line depending on how its incentives operate over time, the legality of a given MLM is a fact-specific question rather than a fixed label.
