Legal Distinction Between MLMs And Illegal Pyramid Schemes

The legal line between a legitimate multi-level marketing company and an illegal pyramid scheme comes down to where the money actually originates. In a lawful MLM, participants earn income primarily by selling a genuine product or service to real end customers, and any commissions on a recruit's activity are tied to those downstream sales. A pyramid scheme, by contrast, generates most of its revenue from the act of recruiting itself — new participants pay to join, and that money flows upward, with little or no real demand for whatever product may nominally be attached. Because the product in a pyramid scheme is often just a token to disguise the recruitment engine, the business collapses once new sign-ups slow, which is why the model is treated as inherently deceptive rather than merely risky.

Regulators focus on this distinction when deciding whether a given company has crossed the line. In the United States, the evaluates factors such as whether compensation is driven by retail sales versus recruitment, whether distributors are pressured to buy large amounts of inventory they cannot resell, and whether the company offers a meaningful buyback policy for unsold stock.. The difficulty is that many MLMs sit in a gray area: they sell real products but design compensation plans that still reward recruiting heavily, leaving courts and regulators to judge each arrangement on its particular structure rather than on the label the company gives itself.
