Recruitment-Versus-Product-Revenue Ratio Checks

A recruitment-versus-product-revenue ratio check is one of the core diagnostics used to tell a legitimate direct-sales company apart from a pyramid scheme. The idea is simple: in a real product business, most money should flow from selling goods or services to genuine end customers, not from signing up new distributors and collecting their starter kits, inventory buy-ins, or ongoing purchase requirements. A scam checker applying this test tries to trace where a company's revenue and a typical participant's earnings actually originate. If income is driven mainly by the act of recruiting people beneath you rather than by moving product to buyers who want it, the compensation structure starts to resemble a pyramid, where early joiners are effectively paid out of the fees of later ones. In practice, the check looks for warning signs that recruitment has crowded out real sales. These include compensation that rewards enrolling new members more than retail transactions, pressure to buy large amounts of inventory to qualify for bonuses (sometimes leaving distributors with unsold stock), and a customer base made up almost entirely of other distributors rather than outside consumers. Because companies rarely publish a clean breakdown of these figures, a scam checker often relies on proxies such as income-disclosure statements, refund and buyback policies, and how heavily marketing emphasizes "building a team" versus the product itself.
