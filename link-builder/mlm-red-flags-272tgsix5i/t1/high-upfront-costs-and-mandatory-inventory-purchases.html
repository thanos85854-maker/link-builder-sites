High Upfront Costs And Mandatory Inventory Purchases

One of the most commonly cited warning signs of a problematic multi-level marketing operation is a steep financial barrier to entry. Legitimate businesses generally let people start earning without demanding a large payment first, whereas some MLMs require new participants to purchase expensive starter kits, training packages, or promotional materials before they can begin selling. When the cost of joining is high and framed as an investment in one's own success, it can pressure recruits into spending significant money up front on the promise of future returns that may never materialize.

Closely related is the practice of mandatory inventory purchases, sometimes called inventory loading or front-loading. Here participants are required, or strongly encouraged, to buy and keep a stock of product on hand to maintain their status, qualify for commissions, or meet monthly purchase quotas. This shifts financial risk onto the individual, who may end up with more merchandise than they can realistically sell. Regulators and consumer advocates treat heavy emphasis on buying inventory, rather than selling to genuine outside customers, as a signal that the compensation may depend more on recruitment and internal purchasing than on real retail demand.
