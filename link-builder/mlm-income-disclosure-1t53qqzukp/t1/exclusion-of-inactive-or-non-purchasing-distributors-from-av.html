Exclusion Of Inactive Or Non-Purchasing Distributors From Averages

One recurring criticism of multi-level marketing income disclosures concerns which participants are counted when average or typical earnings are calculated. Many disclosures report figures based only on distributors classified as "active," and the definition of active can vary widely from one company to another. When people who signed up but did not order product, did not make sales, or did not meet some minimum activity threshold during the measurement period are removed from the pool, they also disappear from the denominator used to compute averages. Because a large share of MLM participants typically fall into these inactive or non-purchasing categories, excluding them tends to raise the reported average earnings and can make the opportunity look more lucrative than the full distributor base would suggest.

The practical effect is that two companies can describe very different realities using the same word "average," depending on where each one draws the line for inclusion. A reader comparing disclosures cannot assume the populations are equivalent, and a figure that omits inactive members says little about the odds a newly enrolled person will earn a comparable amount. For this reason, consumer advocates and regulators generally encourage looking at what portion of all participants — not just active ones — earned nothing or lost money after accounting for their own purchases and expenses.
