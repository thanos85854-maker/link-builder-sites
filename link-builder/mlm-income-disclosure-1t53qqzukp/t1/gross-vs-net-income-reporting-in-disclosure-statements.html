Gross Vs. Net Income Reporting In Disclosure Statements

Income disclosure statements published by multi-level marketing companies frequently report participant earnings on a gross basis, meaning the figures reflect commissions and bonuses paid out before any of the participant's own costs are subtracted. Those costs can be substantial: the price of starter kits, ongoing product purchases or autoship requirements needed to stay eligible for commissions, sales aids, event fees, and shipping. Because a gross figure captures money received without accounting for money spent to earn it, it can present a far rosier picture of a distributor's financial position than what the person actually keeps. Two participants with identical gross earnings can end up in very different places once their expenses are counted.

Net income, by contrast, is what remains after those business expenses are deducted, and it is the number that reflects whether participation was actually profitable. Critics of MLM disclosures often point out that gross reporting, combined with averages that may include inactive or barely active members, tends to obscure how many participants earn little or lose money once costs are factored in. Regulators and consumer advocates have generally encouraged clearer, net-based or cost-inclusive reporting so that prospective recruits can judge realistic outcomes, though disclosure practices vary widely and are not uniformly standardized across companies.
